An Analysis of Quadratic Funding Mechanics and Outcomes in Octant

Octant staked 100,000 ETH and directs the staking yield to fund impactful Ethereum projects through matching grants. Over 2.5 years it has distributed 3,405 ETH to 134 projects with no strings attached.

Key Findings
  • Staking yield provides 96% of payouts to projects.
  • The remaining 4% comes from user donations, but those donations decide where the 96% goes.
  • An average $19 donation in Octant QF brings a project $500 in matching.
  • The more donors, the stronger the matching effect.
  • 10 more donors bring a project +$5,000 in matching on average, regardless of donation size.
  • For example, ZachXBT in Epoch 9 raised $1,100 from 95 donors. The matching pool from staking yield added $128,000 on top. A 116× leverage.

Where AI slop and InfoFi farming reduce humans to traffic counts, this model proves it's possible to build systems where genuine human coordination is the most effective financial multiplier.

Now Octant is shifting from impactful Ethereum projects toward an infrastructure tool open to other ecosystems. Any DAO with a treasury can now use vaults for yield generation and then allocate that yield to funding rounds for its own community.

Mashal, CMO of Octant, said: “Ethereum is the starting point but I see the potential of this technology to be adopted by real world non-crypto organizations too.”

How Octant funding distributes across 9 project categories

To understand what Octant invests in thematically, I split projects into 9 main categories:

  • Ethereum Infrastructure — core protocol, staking, MEV, attestations
  • Developer Tools — compilers, libraries, IDEs, explorers
  • Security & Privacy — Tor, Shutter, identity, allowance management
  • Public Goods Funding — meta-coordination of grants and funding tools
  • Education & Onboarding — educational programs and academies
  • Research & Analytics — data analytics, on-chain investigations
  • Media & Storytelling — content, journalism, documentary
  • Community & Coordination — DAO governance, community building
  • Climate & Regenerative — climate tech, ReFi, environmental

73% of all Octant funding went to five categories of the technical Ethereum stack. These categories also have the largest average payouts per project, up to 74 ETH in Ethereum Infrastructure versus 6 ETH in Media & Storytelling.

Within this top five, the order changes depending on whether Epoch 0 is included. By absolute volume, Public Goods Funding takes first place with 744 ETH (21.9% of total), but more than half of that comes from the pre-launch round, where Gitcoin, Giveth, Clr.fund, DAO Drops, and Drips took 417 of 535 ETH. Without Epoch 0, Ethereum Infrastructure and Developer Tools take the lead, having received matching consistently across all V1 epochs.

Themed rounds had the opposite effect, more projects and less money for each. Climate & Regenerative (23 projects from the Epoch 7 climate round) and Media & Storytelling (26 projects from the Epoch 9 Ethereum Stories round) together make up 37% of all unique projects in the data, but received only 13.6% of total funding. The average check was 13 ETH per climate project and 6 ETH per media project versus 74 ETH in Ethereum Infrastructure.

How the switch to Quadratic Funding changed allocations

Before the fourth epoch, matching funding in Octant used a fixed pool split between projects proportionally to how much each received in donations. In the first epoch the multiplier was 44.4× for all projects, in the second 58.4×, in the third 14.3×. One person donating $100 and one hundred people donating $1 each produced the same result.

Starting from the fourth epoch, Octant launched Quadratic Funding, where the number of donors per project began to matter significantly. The broader the donor base, the larger the share of the pool a project receives. One hundred people donating $1 each now give a project significantly more matching than one person donating $100.

On average, one additional donor making a $19 donation shifts $500 of the matching pool toward the project.

USD figures from here on are ETH converted at $3,000 per ETH, an average across epochs 4 to 11. The real rate over that period ranged from $1,600 to $4,200.

That is, the average donor brings a project 26 times more money than they contributed themselves.

On average, a project with 10 more unique donors received +$5,000 in matching, regardless of donation size.

How donors participate across Octant’s epochs

Across 11 epochs, 1,270 unique donors participated in Octant. 61% of them came back for two or more epochs. 11 people took part in every one of the 11 epochs, and 22 more missed only one.

Most donors come once. 39% of donors take part in a single round and never return. At the other end, 33 people showed up in 10 or more of the 11 epochs. The number of donors at each level of participation drops sharply between these two extremes.

96% of payouts come from staking yield, 4% from user donations

Donors contributed 109.51 ETH of their personal rewards directly to projects. On top of that, the matching pool from Octant’s staking yield added 2,760 ETH. So final payouts to projects break down as 96% staking yield contribution and 4% user allocations, which decide where the yield goes.

Appendix

Methodology

I started by wanting to assemble a complete list of projects broken down by epoch, with exact ETH amounts (allocated and matched separately) and recipient addresses, so I could compare them and split them into categories. Octant publishes aggregated numbers per epoch in its docs and dashboard, plus a leaderboard for each epoch separately, but I couldn’t find a single dataset covering all projects with precise amounts across the entire history. I had to put it together myself.

I started by opening the Octant app in a browser, switching epochs in DevTools Network tab, and catching the XHR requests that pull the leaderboard. The base URL turned out to be backend.mainnet.octant.app. I opened /openapi.json and got the Swagger spec with all V1 API endpoints. The API is public, no auth, runs on top of an Ethereum subgraph. I wrote a Python script hitting three relevant endpoints (per-epoch leaderboard, aggregate stats, address-to-name mapping), ran it across all 12 epochs, and saved the JSON.

All epochs except Epoch 0 returned clean data. Epoch 0 was missing from the API because it was a pre-launch curated round before the V1 mechanics went live, and it doesn’t fit the API schema.

A small note on numbering. Octant counts epochs from zero. There have been 12 rounds total, from Epoch 0 to Epoch 11. The current launch starting in June 2026, the first on the new platform version, is called Epoch 12.

To close the Epoch 0 gap, I found the Octant distribution wallet in the docs (0x70a0a7be87deb51e1fab16d4f2bf00be1510e476) and pulled all its transactions via Python. That filled in the missing data and let me cross-validate between the two sources.

To consolidate the data, I merged the V1 API data (epochs 1–11) and the on-chain reconstruction (Epoch 0) into a single master CSV, which gave me 273 rows at the project-epoch level. Then I grouped by project and normalized names, because across epochs Octant occasionally wrote the same project with variations like “Tor Project” versus “The Tor Project” or “Hypercerts” versus “Hypercerts Foundation”. I collapsed all such cases to a single canonical name. The result was a cumulative leaderboard of 134 unique projects with total funding across the full history of Octant.

Next I wanted to understand what Octant invests in thematically, but 134 names without grouping are hard to interpret. I needed categorization. I went through the list and assigned each project one of 9 categories:

  • Ethereum Infrastructure — core protocol, staking, MEV, attestations
  • Developer Tools — compilers, libraries, IDEs, explorers
  • Security & Privacy — Tor, Shutter, identity, allowance management
  • Public Goods Funding — meta-coordination of grants and funding tools
  • Education & Onboarding — educational programs and academies
  • Research & Analytics — data analytics, on-chain investigations
  • Media & Storytelling — content, journalism, documentary
  • Community & Coordination — DAO governance, community building
  • Climate & Regenerative — climate tech, ReFi, environmental

Categorization was manual. I went through 134 projects, either already knowing the topic or looking at the project’s website. Each got one of the 9 categories. Borderline cases (for example, a project that was both a security tool and a DAO governance system) were resolved by primary function. After that I could see which categories dominate by funding volume and by average check per project.

Then I wanted to understand how allocations work and who these donors are. The Octant V1 API has an endpoint /allocations/project/{address}/epoch/{N} that returns all user donations to a specific project in a specific epoch with individual donor addresses. I ran this endpoint across all 263 project-epoch combinations in V1 (epochs 1–11) and assembled a dataset of 17,595 individual allocations. Of these, 11,702 allocations came from the 195 project-epochs in QF rounds (E4–E11), which are the basis for the leverage analysis. Each record contains a donor address, project address, epoch, and amount in ETH.

All donor count metrics assume Gitcoin Passport effectively filters duplicate addresses across the donor base. Octant publishes per-epoch sybil reports which were not independently audited in this analysis.

For retention stats, I grouped allocations by donor address and counted how many unique epochs each donor participated in. This gave 1,270 unique donors with participation distribution from 1 to 11 epochs. Recurring donors (2+ epochs) come to 773 people or 60.9%. Core base (10+ epochs) is 33. Perfect attendance across all 11 epochs is 11 people.

To trace how the matching mechanism evolved across epochs, I calculated the match/allocated ratio for every project in every epoch. In epochs 1–3 this ratio was the same for every project within an epoch (44.4× in the first, 58.4× in the second, 14.3× in the third). That confirmed Octant used proportional matching before launching QF. From the fourth epoch on, the ratio varies per project depending on donor count, and that variation is the main mechanical effect of QF. Specific examples (Solidity versus hevm in Epoch 8, ZachXBT in Epoch 9) came straight from the master dataset by project address.

Match-per-donor analysis I did in two passes. First, a simple version. For each QF project-epoch (194 rows) I calculated avg donation size (allocated / n_donors) and match per donor (matched / n_donors). Bucketing project-epochs by average donation size, I got leverage of 93× for donations under $2 down to 9× for donations above $50. Then a weighted version, weighted by donor count across all 11,702 individual allocations in QF epochs. That gives $19 average donation, $500 average match per donor, 26× leverage. The weighted version reflects the typical donor experience rather than the typical project experience, and that’s what I used for the main claims in the article.

User-versus-matching-pool split I calculated by summing. All allocated_eth across V1 epochs comes to 109.51 ETH (the portion of staking yield that flowed to users as personal rewards and that they directed to projects instead of keeping for themselves). All matched_eth comes to 2,760 ETH (the portion of staking yield that went straight into the matching pool). Total V1 funding to projects is 2,870 ETH. The split is 96/4 in favor of the matching pool.

For USD conversions I used $3,000 per ETH as a rough average across QF epochs (August 2023 to January 2026). The real price ranged $1,600–$4,200. This isn’t critical for directional analysis (leverage ratios, distribution patterns), but it’s worth keeping in mind when interpreting specific USD numbers like “$500 average match per donor” or “$128,000 ZachXBT matching”.

Limitations

The donor count metrics assume Gitcoin Passport effectively filters duplicate addresses. Octant publishes per-epoch sybil reports, but those reports were not independently verified here. Individual leverage figures may be sensitive to sybil noise even if aggregate patterns hold.

The relationship between donor base size and matching allocation is mechanical (a property of the QF formula) but the causal direction is not testable from observational data alone. Projects with broad donor bases may differ from projects with narrow bases on other dimensions such as community recognition, project age, or external mobilization capacity, which are not controlled for in this analysis.

USD figures use $3,000 per ETH as a reference average. The real ETH price ranged $1,600 to $4,200 across the observed period, so specific USD figures carry meaningful variance. Leverage ratios and distribution patterns are unaffected by this choice.

Octant V2 mechanics are described from publicly available materials but not analyzed empirically here, since V2 launches in June 2026 after the V1 dataset closed.
