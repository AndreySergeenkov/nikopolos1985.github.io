Glass effect — technique comparison

The same card rendered six ways. Move/scroll over the colourful backdrop to see how each one bends, blurs and lights what's behind it. C, D and F rely on SVG-filter backdrops (Chromium); A and E work everywhere.

A — Frosted

Pure CSS glassmorphism

Just backdrop-filter: blur() saturate() over a translucent tint. No refraction — only a clean frost.

Support: all modern browsers · Cost: cheap

Safest choice

B — Liquid ripple

Fractal-noise displacement

Turbulence drives feDisplacementMap, wobbling the backdrop like water across the whole surface.

Support: Chromium · Cost: medium

Playful / watery

C — Edge refraction

Gradient lens map

A grey-centre gradient displacement map bends only the rim — the realistic "thick bevel" of true glass.

Support: Chromium · Cost: medium

Most realistic

D — Chromatic

RGB channel split

Each colour channel is displaced differently, giving the coloured edge fringing of dispersive glass.

Support: Chromium · Cost: higher

Most "premium"

E — No-filter fallback

Gradients + shadows only

Fakes glass with layered highlights and shadows. Doesn't truly refract, but renders identically everywhere.

Support: universal · Cost: cheapest

Bulletproof

F — Specular rim

SVG lighting

feSpecularLighting builds a physically-lit bright edge from surface normals — a glossy lit bevel.

Support: Chromium · Cost: higher

Glossy / lit

Picking one for the wiki site: on a near-white page there's little colour behind the glass to refract, so the dramatic options (C/D/F) show mostly as a faint shimmer. For that context A (Frosted) or C (Edge refraction) with a very subtle backdrop read best and stay legible. The flashy variants shine on rich, photographic backgrounds like this lab.
